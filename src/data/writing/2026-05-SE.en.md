I think we can learn a lot from yesterday's phones. Many of the qualities I miss in today's models can be found in older devices, like my iPhone SE (2016).

---

## Size

The screen is very small compared to today's phones;

**iPhone SE (2016)**:
- Screen size ~10.2cm (4")
- 60.8% screen-to-body ratio
**iPhone 16 Pro (2024)**:
- Screen size ~16cm (6.3")
- 90.1% screen-to-body ratio

I see a lot of advantages in this:
- The phone fits in any pocket
- Everything can be controlled easily with one hand
- The phone is very light

My daily driver, a 16 Pro, on the other hand requires both hands or a death grip to reach the top of the screen.

## The 3.5mm jack

A classic debate. Over the past couple of years I've experimented with all types of headphones; IEMs, open-back, closed-back, AirPods, EarPods, over-ear Bluetooth. Nothing works better than wired headphones. They weigh nothing, cost less, and if you know [how to coil a cable properly](https://youtu.be/yL-VBLAGLdc?si=5JG3qXGKp9-AG3Sf), they'll last longer than most Bluetooth headphones. Microphone quality is also noticeably better when I make calls.

## The Touch ID home button

Touch ID is possibly one of the most intuitive and least fragile ways to authenticate. Quickly placing your thumb on the home button is pure muscle memory. It's also a natural resting position for the thumb, which gives a better grip. In my experience, Face ID has a few weaknesses.

It occasionally requires a second look, and the phone won't unlock when I have a pillow in front of my face. I understand the ideology behind Face ID; that it just works. Unfortunately we're not quite there yet. A phone is a tool, and the fact that it demands my full attention dozens of times a day is annoying.

## The camera is good enough

In everyday use, the SE's camera does its job. For those who want higher quality, the camera can shoot 4K 30fps, though this isn't recommended due to limited storage. The two features I personally use most are the zoom and the aspect ratios (16:9, 1:1).

Older Apple models also apply significantly less processing to photos. This was a real problem on my old Google Pixel 7a: as soon as I opened the photo gallery, the over-processed version of the image would fade in, with no option to turn it off in the settings.

## Is your phone a distraction?

I think the worse the phone is, the better it is. That's probably not a popular opinion. With 60Hz and a small screen, you do what you need to do and put it down. ProMotion keeps me on longer than I planned.

It's fairly common knowledge today that phones, apps, websites and so on are purpose-built to take up as much of your time as possible. I've felt that myself. I won't say the SE is a direct solution to this addiction problem, but it's a step in the right direction.

## Cheap spare parts

I've replaced the screen on my SE four times so far, partly because the repair process has a low barrier and takes 45 minutes, and partly because the phone is so old. Spare parts are very cheap even with Norwegian import duties (around 450 NOK). The battery I've replaced once.

## What I miss

Let me take off my rose-tinted glasses for a moment; the SE's battery life and performance are generally much worse. Everything feels more cramped, and there have been a couple of times the hardware has let me down when I needed it right then and there. A smaller screen also squeezes the keyboard together, making it hard to type as fast as on the 16 Pro. Still, I'm able to look past these issues. If a smaller alternative to today's large smartphones with a modern operating system came along, I'd buy it immediately.

## Alternatives

Such alternatives do actually exist:
- Light Phone II and III
- Clicks Communicator
- Punkt MP02
- Boox Palma 2
- The Minimal Phone

The downside is usually short-lived manufacturer support (through updates, warranty claims, etc.), and security concerns. Many of these are also laughably overpriced relative to what you get for your money.

## Conclusion

The SE isn't perfect, but that's in many ways the point. It does what a phone should, without making it easy to disappear into something else. The compromises are there: shorter battery life, slower performance, a cramped keyboard. But it's a phone I use with intention, not one I fall into without noticing.

We live in a time when technology is optimised to hold our attention for as long as possible. Choosing a device that _doesn't_ do that is a personal act of rebellion against it. The SE reminds me that tools should serve us, not the other way around.

:::callout[Takeaway]
A good phone doesn't need to be the best. It just needs to stay in the background and let you live your life in front of it.
:::
